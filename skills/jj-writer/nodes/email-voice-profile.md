# Jarad Johnson Email Voice Profile

Derived from Jarad's private sent-email corpus from 2025-04-26 through 2026-04-26. The raw corpus stays local-only at `~/.config/dot/private/voice/sent-email-corpus/2025-04-26_to_2026-04-26/`.

Use this with `jarad-style-guide.md` and `voice-dna.md` when drafting or rewriting email from Jarad. This is the channel layer. The general voice still matters, but email has a different job: get the point across, make the next step easy, and sound like a real person who knows why he is writing.

## Core Pattern

Jarad's email voice is practical, warm when earned, and usually pointed at the next useful move. It does not perform professionalism. It sounds like a capable operator trying to make the work easier for the person reading it.

The surface is casual. The intent is precise. Most emails do one of 4 things:

1. acknowledge what happened,
2. give a clear read,
3. name the next step,
4. make it easy for the other person to respond.

## Openings

Use `Hi [Name],` for normal client/professional emails.

Use `Hey [Name],` when there is familiarity, warmth, or a lighter touch.

For quick replies, it is normal to skip ceremony and start with:

- `Sounds great.`
- `Got it.`
- `Perfect.`
- `Thanks for the update.`
- `Just wanted to follow up...`

Avoid inflated openings like `I hope this message finds you well`, `I am thrilled to connect`, or `Thank you for the opportunity to partner`. If a warm opener is needed, ground it in a real fact.

## Paragraph Rhythm

Default to short paragraphs. Most emails are 2-6 short paragraphs with one idea each.

A strong Jarad email often does this:

- greeting,
- one sentence acknowledging context,
- one paragraph with the actual point,
- one explicit ask or next step,
- simple closing.

Longer emails are still plain. Use bullets only when they make logistics easier: dates, time slots, questions, or itemized feedback. Do not turn an email into a memo unless the situation demands it.

## Warmth

Warmth should be specific. Generic warmth sounds fake fast.

Good:

- `It meant a lot.`
- `I appreciate the work you're doing.`
- `That info is super valuable.`
- `Love this thing.`

Weak:

- `This is exactly the kind of opportunity we are passionate about.`
- `We are excited to explore synergies.`
- `Your organization is doing incredible work.`

If the email includes praise, make it point to a real detail: the event, the work, the feedback, the relationship, the gift, the meeting, or the specific thing they sent.

## Asks and Next Steps

Make the ask obvious and low-friction. Do not make the reader hunt for the action.

Common moves:

- `Let me know if...`
- `Do any of these slots work for you?`
- `Could you share...?`
- `Would Monday work?`
- `If I have it wrong, please correct me.`
- `I'll follow up when I'm back.`

Jarad often gives the other person an easy path: a short call, a few time windows, a teammate copied in, or a concrete document to review.

## Follow-Ups

Follow-ups should be calm and non-needy.

Use:

- `Just wanted to follow up to see if you've had a chance to review...`
- `Want to make sure I didn't miss it in spam.`
- `Let me know if a quick call would help clarify anything.`

Do not add artificial urgency. Do not over-explain why you are following up. One clear nudge is enough.

## Pushback and Disagreement

Jarad's pushback is direct but not theatrical. He usually explains the practical concern, then names the next move.

Use:

- `I don't see any concerns on our end.`
- `I think we can move quite a bit faster if...`
- `This is something I'm currently opposed to.`
- `I don't trust AIs to hold accurate and aligned views across that much content.`
- `Friday is looking ambitious right now.`

Avoid:

- fake diplomacy that hides the point,
- aggressive certainty,
- legalistic caveats unless legal review is actually the topic,
- long preambles before the concern.

## Sales and Client Tone

The sales voice is grounded and useful. It should sound like Mostly Serious can help because of specific experience, not because of a generic claim.

Good pattern:

- acknowledge the need,
- connect it to relevant work,
- introduce the right teammate,
- propose a discovery call or next step.

Jarad can say `happy to` in email, but use it as a utility phrase, not as assistant-flavored politeness. `Happy to jump on a call` is fine. `I'd be happy to assist you with that` is not.

## Closings

Most common closings:

- `Cheers,`
- `Thanks!`
- `Thanks,`
- `Talk soon,`
- `Have a great weekend!`

Then sign `Jarad`.

Use `Cheers,` as the default for normal professional warmth. Use `Thanks!` when the email has an ask or a quick handoff. Use no formal closing only for very short internal replies.

## Red Flags

An email is drifting out of Jarad's voice if it:

- sounds like a brand instead of a person,
- opens with generic warmth,
- praises without a concrete reason,
- buries the ask,
- uses corporate filler like `leverage`, `synergy`, `seamless`, `robust`, or `transformative`,
- over-apologizes,
- turns a simple next step into a polished paragraph,
- uses `I believe` where `I think` would sound more natural,
- says `we are excited` without naming why.

## Write-To Card

When drafting Jarad email, aim for this:

> Friendly operator. Short paragraphs. Direct read. Specific warmth. Clear next step. Normal human closing.
