## Core Thesis

Acemoglu is the standing skeptic on AI's automatic upside. He argues that AI's
aggregate impact depends on which tasks are affected, what cost savings are
real, and whether deployment shifts income toward capital while creating low-
value or socially harmful tasks. His frame is not anti-technology; it is that
institutions and the direction of innovation determine whether AI becomes
pro-worker or merely so-so automation with distributional downside.

Sources:
- https://www.nber.org/papers/w32487
- https://news.mit.edu/2024/3-questions-shaping-future-work-age-of-ai-0228
- https://economics.mit.edu/people/faculty/daron-acemoglu

## Key Frameworks

- Task-based macroeconomics of AI: macro gains are constrained by the
  share of tasks affected and the size of task-level savings.
- Direction of technology: policy, institutions, and norms shape whether
  technology serves prosperity broadly or concentrates power.
- Power and Progress: technological progress has to be judged by who gains,
  who loses, and what kinds of tasks are being created.

## Recent Work (2023+)

### The Simple Macroeconomics of AI (2024, NBER)
- URL: https://www.nber.org/papers/w32487
- Summary: Acemoglu's cleanest recent statement of his AI growth and
  distribution lens.
- Key claim: Near-term macro effects are real but modest, and capital-labor
  inequality can still widen.

### 3 Questions: Shaping the future of work in an age of AI (2024, MIT News)
- URL: https://news.mit.edu/2024/3-questions-shaping-future-work-age-of-ai-0228
- Summary: Public explanation of the MIT initiative's research and policy
  agenda.
- Key claim: AI needs a coherent framework that identifies the inefficiencies
  and misdirections of technology.

### Power and Progress (2023, cited on MIT profile)
- URL: https://economics.mit.edu/people/faculty/daron-acemoglu
- Summary: MIT profile lists the book as a current part of Acemoglu's
  technology-and-prosperity work.
- Key claim: The relevant question is not whether progress exists, but who it
  serves and what political economy shapes its direction.

## Methodological Style

Acemoglu combines theory, political economy, historical comparison, and
empirical work on tasks and institutions. In the AI context he tends to
translate broad claims into tractable macro constraints rather than taking
headline productivity narratives at face value.

Sources:
- https://www.nber.org/papers/w32487
- https://news.mit.edu/2024/3-questions-shaping-future-work-age-of-ai-0228
- https://economics.mit.edu/people/faculty/daron-acemoglu

## Ideological Position

Institutionalist, skeptical of laissez-faire AI, and consistently focused on
worker power, distribution, and the governance of technological direction. He
is open to pro-worker AI, but does not assume markets will produce it on their
own.

Sources:
- https://www.nber.org/papers/w32487
- https://news.mit.edu/2024/3-questions-shaping-future-work-age-of-ai-0228

## Where Others Push Back

- Erik Brynjolfsson: sees larger realized productivity gains in current
  deployments and more upside from augmentation-first design.
- Philippe Aghion: is more willing to say AI can raise employment at
  adopting firms when tasks are complementary and policy stays pro-competition.
- Andrew McAfee: is much more worried about under-adoption than about
  overestimating gains.
