## Core Thesis

Autor treats AI as a choice about what happens to expertise, not as a simple
end-of-work story. His recent AI work argues that the key question is whether
new systems extend skilled judgment into more jobs or strip judgment out of
work and commodify labor. He is explicitly pro-AI when it raises the value of
human expertise and creates new productive tasks, and explicitly skeptical when
deployment is just faster substitution.

## Key Frameworks

- **Expertise extension** - the real labor question is whether AI augments the
  value of human expertise or makes judgment valueless.
- **Direction of technology is chosen** - technological trajectories are shaped
  by institutions, policy, and organizational choices rather than arriving as
  fate.
- **Middle-skill rebuilding** - decision-support tools can let less-formally
  credentialed workers perform more expert tasks.

## Methodological Style

Autor works from task-based labor economics, occupational comparison, and
historical labor-market evidence, then translates that evidence into concrete
institutional design questions. His recent AI output mixes public testimony,
initiative memos, and applied examples rather than abstract futurism alone.

## Ideological Position

Pro-worker and explicitly interventionist on the direction of technology.
Autor's recent framing is not anti-innovation; it is anti-fatalism and anti-
deployment that hollows out judgment while treating resulting inequality as
inevitable.

## Where Others Push Back

- **Daron Acemoglu** - more skeptical about aggregate productivity gains and
  more explicit that AI can widen the capital-versus-labor split even when some
  tasks get easier.
- **Andrew McAfee** - more bullish on rapid adoption, competitiveness, and the
  cost of underusing AI.
- **Philippe Aghion** - more optimistic that complementary-task deployment can
  raise employment at adopting firms, even if some job families are exposed.
