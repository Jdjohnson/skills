## Core Thesis

Furman brings the policy-realism lane. His recent AI writing treats the
technology as promising but regulation-sensitive, argues that policymakers
should weigh both the risks of AI and the risks of slower AI development, and
prefers decentralized sector expertise over one giant AI regulator.

Sources:
- https://www.hks.harvard.edu/faculty/jason-furman
- https://www.nber.org/chapters/c15125.pdf
- https://www.hks.harvard.edu/centers/mrcbg/publications/ai-not-something-we-should-be-afraid

## Key Frameworks

- Benefit-cost regulation: AI governance should weigh lost upside as well
  as direct harms.
- Compare AI with humans: policy should benchmark AI against the actual
  human alternative, not perfection.
- Sector-specific oversight: domain experts beat a single all-purpose AI
  regulator.

## Recent Work (2023+)

### AI 'not something we should be afraid of' (2024, Harvard Kennedy School)
- URL: https://www.hks.harvard.edu/centers/mrcbg/publications/ai-not-something-we-should-be-afraid
- Summary: HKS excerpt of Furman discussing AI regulation and social adaptation.
- Key claim: The goal of regulation should be enabling more useful AI while
  handling downstream adjustment problems.

### Regulating AI: Six Principles and Their Consequences (2025, NBER chapter)
- URL: https://www.nber.org/chapters/c15125.pdf
- Summary: Furman's clearest recent statement of his AI-governance framework.
- Key claim: Regulators should prefer benefit-cost analysis to a pure
  precautionary principle.
