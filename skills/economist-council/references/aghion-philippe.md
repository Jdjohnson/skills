## Core Thesis

Aghion's AI lens is Schumpeterian: growth comes from innovation, creative
destruction, and competition, not from static equilibrium alone. In his recent
AI work he is more optimistic than Acemoglu about growth and, in some cases,
employment, provided AI substitutes for some tasks while raising the value of
others. He still expects uneven exposure across occupations, which is why he
keeps returning to policy, competition, and complementary-task design.

Sources:
- https://www.frbsf.org/news-and-media/events/2024/04/philippe-aghion-the-growth-and-employment-effects-of-ai/
- https://www.frbsf.org/wp-content/uploads/AI-and-Growth-Aghion-Bunel.pdf

## Key Frameworks

- Schumpeterian growth: innovation and creative destruction drive long-run
  productivity growth.
- Task complementarity: AI can improve jobs when it replaces narrower task
  bundles and frees workers for higher-value work.
- Competition and policy: diffusion, firm incentives, and competition
  policy shape whether AI's gains spread.

## Methodological Style

Aghion is theory-forward but not theory-only. His recent AI work pairs
Schumpeterian growth logic with firm- and task-level evidence and translates the
result into competition and innovation-policy implications.

Sources:
- https://www.frbsf.org/news-and-media/events/2024/04/philippe-aghion-the-growth-and-employment-effects-of-ai/
- https://www.frbsf.org/wp-content/uploads/AI-and-Growth-Aghion-Bunel.pdf

## Ideological Position

Pro-innovation, pro-competition, and comfortable with an active state that
supports diffusion and guards against concentration. He is the least allergic in
this panel to saying AI can raise growth and employment together under the
right conditions.

## Where Others Push Back

- Daron Acemoglu: much smaller near-term macro estimates and a stronger
  warning about capital taking the upside.
- David Autor: more focused on whether expertise broadens in a pro-worker
  direction than on growth arithmetic alone.
- Diane Coyle: would remind that even real gains can be slow to show up in
  official statistics.
