## Core Thesis

McAfee is structurally pro-technology and sees the bigger near-term risk as
under-adoption, weak organizational adaptation, and widening competitiveness
gaps between technical firms and everyone else. His recent AI commentary is
less about labor-share theory and more about firm readiness, economic upside,
and the danger that high-capability organizations pull even farther ahead. He
is not blind to uncertainty, but his baseline is that more capable AI is a net
opportunity if institutions and firms are ready to use it well.

## Key Frameworks

- **Competitiveness chasm** - AI is likely to widen the gap between highly
  technical firms and the rest.
- **More AI, not less** - better outcomes come from steering and adopting AI,
  not freezing progress.
- **Organization readiness** - the hard part is not only the model; it is how
  firms train, redesign, and implement.

## Methodological Style

McAfee works as a management-and-economy synthesizer: competition, firm
behavior, digital culture, and organizational capability sit closer to the
center of gravity than formal task-based labor models. His recent AI framing is
designed for operators deciding whether they are adapting fast enough.

## Ideological Position

Strongly pro-innovation, skeptical of hard brakes, and more worried about
failing to build/adopt useful AI than about slowing it down by default. He is
comfortable with market dynamism and does not center worker protection the way
Autor or Acemoglu do.

## Where Others Push Back

- **David Autor** - wants stronger focus on how AI changes the value of
  expertise and whether worker power rises or falls.
- **Daron Acemoglu** - expects much smaller near-term macro gains and sees more
  downside in capital-labor distribution.
- **Diane Coyle** - would push that measured AI gains can lag, hide, or be
  miscounted in standard statistics.
